\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Lerna is a Hydra-native discipline for representing many virtual-channel
rights compactly inside a Hydra Head or an Interhead virtual Head, and for
deterministically materialising those rights when optimistic updating stops.
It is not a replacement for Hydra close, contest, or fanout. It sits beneath
those mechanisms and supplies the missing inner rule: compact factory rights
must be turned into ordinary Head UTxOs or claim UTxOs before they can be
safely exposed to the outer settlement layer. The paper presents one protocol
with two settlement profiles. The Head-local factory profile keeps factory
rights inside the Head ledger and is safe only when the latest Lerna state is
embedded in signed snapshot evidence before close or available for contest.
The claim-materialisation profile makes that repayment explicit through a
ledger-enforced claim object, a deterministic transition relation, bounded
admission rules, verifiable evidence binding, and a conditional acceptance
theorem.

\medskip
\noindent
The design is intentionally bounded. Lerna does not compete with Interhead
Hydra's virtualisation of Heads across Heads, nor with Hydrozoa's broader
execution/custody separation and deterministic outer rollout. Lerna uses those
works as boundary conditions: Interhead explains where virtual Heads may live
and why enforceable state must survive failed cooperation; Hydrozoa shows why
compact off-chain state must have deterministic payout order, fallback
discipline, and no stranded value when custody is at stake. Hydra Head supplies
the close/contest/fanout substrate and the practical pressure that makes
compactness valuable. Lerna's originality is narrower and more internal: an
inner factory-rights discipline with explicit host-interface obligations,
deterministic materialisation, non-interference, release receipts, balance
preservation, and no-stranding. The claim-materialisation profile is conditional
on Hydra contestability, ledger validation of submitted transactions, proof
availability, watcher/operator liveness, and materialisation capacity inside the
committed budget. If the Head-local liveness assumption is unacceptable, the
claim-materialisation profile is the appropriate safety envelope.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Hydra Head gives a fixed participant set a Cardano-like off-chain ledger:
participants deposit UTxOs, sign snapshots, close with a snapshot when
necessary, contest stale closes, and fan out the resolved UTxO set to
L1~\cite{hydra-paper,hydra-docs}. This is already a powerful settlement
substrate. A virtual-channel factory layered inside or near a Head should
therefore avoid inventing an unmeasured parallel dispute game. Its hard problem
is narrower and more operational: when compact rights are no longer being
updated optimistically, what exact inner object or transition discipline turns
them back into ordinary ledger claims without double release, stale-state
settlement, or silent asset loss?

Lerna answers that question as a factory-rights protocol with two safety
envelopes. In the Head-local factory profile, compact rights remain inside the
Head or virtual Head and are safe only if the latest Lerna state is already
embedded in a signed Hydra snapshot before close, or if an honest actor can
contest a stale close with such a newer signed snapshot. In the
claim-materialisation profile, the same repayment is made explicit by a
ledger-controlled claim object carrying unresolved value, latest-state metadata,
authorisation policy, release-receipt state, proof-availability commitments, and
materialisation bounds. A final settlement consumes all unresolved claim value.
A partial settlement creates a continuation claim with a strictly advanced
settled-right frontier.

This paper keeps those two profiles in one theory without ordering them as a
delivery story. Claim-materialisation is not presented as a Hydra replacement, a
Hydrozoa competitor, or an ``eltoo for Cardano''. It is the stronger safety
envelope for deployments that cannot rely solely on Head-local snapshot
availability. Instantiation evidence is useful as corroboration, but the thesis
is stated at the level of ledger objects, transition invariants, and acceptance
conditions rather than at the level of a particular codebase.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
The paper makes four protocol contributions.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.6cm,style=nextline]
\item[C1. Inner factory rights discipline.]
Lerna defines the compact object that lives below Hydra fanout: a factory-local
rights state binding factory identity, Hydra Head identity, network id,
settlement epoch, latest-state number, unresolved value, signer policy,
release-receipt root, proof-availability root, and materialisation bounds.
The same discipline can be represented as Head UTxOs in the Head-local profile
or as a claim datum in the claim-materialisation profile
(\Cref{sec:state}).

\item[C2. Deterministic inner materialisation before outer fanout or rollout.]
Lerna makes compactness payable. Before the outer Hydra fanout, Interhead
movement, or Hydrozoa-style rollout is relied upon for custody, compact factory
rights must be deterministically expanded into ordinary ledger outputs or into
a continuation object with an explicitly bounded remaining value
(\Cref{sec:transition,sec:enforcement}).

\item[C3. Factory-local non-interference plus release receipts.]
Lerna separates authorised partial settlement from unauthorised interference.
Reduced-signature materialisation is admissible only with a factory-local frame
proof that untouched owners remain untouched. Each released right also receives
a deterministic receipt, so the protocol has both a non-interference argument
and a nullifier against double release
(\Cref{lem:noninterference,lem:receipt-frontier}).

\item[C4. Host-interface and no-stranding obligations.]
Lerna states what it needs from the surrounding Head or ledger without turning
that host into part of the Lerna protocol: scoped identities, authenticated
state evidence, output accounting, validity deadlines, bounded transaction
capacity, and a way to submit admissible materialisation. Under those
obligations, an accepted transition either pays selected rights or preserves the
remaining value in one bounded continuation; it does not leave compact rights in
an unreachable or ambiguous state
(\Cref{sec:host-interface,lem:no-stranding}).
\end{description}

\paragraph{Claim status.}
This document is a protocol thesis, not a complete proof of every deployment
setting. \textbf{Definitions} specify the intended ledger objects and transition
interfaces. \textbf{Assumptions} state the external conditions Lerna does not
force by itself. \textbf{Lemmas} state local safety facts under those
assumptions. \textbf{\Cref{thm:claim-materialisation-safety}} is a narrow
acceptance theorem for the claim-materialisation profile only; it should not be
read as a theorem for arbitrary Head-local liveness, arbitrary application
semantics, or cross-Head composition.

\paragraph{Design principles.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=P\arabic*.,leftmargin=1.2cm]
\item \textbf{Compactness is debt.} A compact factory object is admissible only
because the protocol defines how it becomes ordinary ledger claims again.
\item \textbf{Outer settlement is not inner materialisation.} Hydra fanout,
Interhead movement, and Hydrozoa-style rollout should see materialised rights or
a bounded continuation, not an opaque unresolved factory.
\item \textbf{Same-scope supersession only.} Latest-state replacement is valid
only inside one domain, network, Head, factory, and settlement epoch.
\item \textbf{Conservation is exact.} Lovelace and native assets must be
conserved across selected outputs and any continuation.
\item \textbf{Locality is a proof obligation.} Reduced-signature settlement is
safe only when the touched rights and untouched frame are explicit.
\item \textbf{Receipts are nullifiers.} Every materialised right advances a
receipt root and frontier so the same right prefix cannot be settled twice.
\item \textbf{Assumptions are first-class.} Proof availability, watcher/operator
liveness, and capacity bounds must be stated where the protocol cannot enforce
them.
\item \textbf{Fallback preserves enforceable state.} If cooperation stops, the
protocol must move either to signed Head evidence or to a ledger-visible claim;
it may not strand rights in an opaque local record.
\item \textbf{Deterministic order beats discretionary payout.} Multi-batch
materialisation must use canonical ordering and a monotone frontier so the next
claim state is independent of operator preference.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem Statement and Design Thesis}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{The Compactness Debt Problem}
\label{sec:compactness-debt}

The central problem is not how to make a Hydra Head hold fewer UTxOs. That is
only the local optimisation. The harder problem is how to make the compression
honest at the moment when cooperative updating stops. A compact factory object
stands for many rights, owners, release conditions, and asset claims. If that
object reaches close, fanout, or outer rollout without a deterministic
materialisation discipline, the host ledger sees only a small object while users
expect individual claims.

Lerna treats this as a compactness debt. The debt is paid when the compact
factory state is expanded into ordinary ledger outputs, release receipts, and,
where necessary, one bounded continuation. This framing explains why
\Cref{sec:state} defines the compact state before \Cref{sec:transition} defines
materialisation, and why \Cref{sec:acceptance} proves only the
claim-materialisation envelope rather than the whole family of possible host
systems.

\subsection{Design Thesis}
\label{sec:design-thesis}

The central thesis is:
\begin{quote}
\emph{A Hydra-native factory is safe only when compact rights are treated as
ledger debt that must be deterministically materialised under explicit
snapshot, evidence, and capacity assumptions.}
\end{quote}

This thesis separates three questions that are often collapsed. First, where is
the latest compact factory state authenticated? Second, who is authorised to
materialise which rights? Third, what exact ledger value must appear after
materialisation? The Head-local profile answers the first question through
signed Head snapshots. The claim-materialisation profile answers it through a
claim object at the ledger boundary. Both profiles rely on the same inner
discipline: same-scope supersession, exact conservation, non-interference for
partial settlement, release receipts, and bounded continuation.

\subsection{Lessons from the Host References}
\label{sec:host-lessons}

The two host references used by this thesis point to the same design demand from
different directions. Interhead treats virtual Head state as enforceable state
that must survive failed cooperation by moving into a ledger-enforced path when
necessary~\cite{interhead}. Hydrozoa separates ordinary cooperative operation
from a fallback regime and gives deterministic rollout rules so large L2
withdrawal sets are paid without stranding value~\cite{hydrozoa-spec}. Lerna
adapts those lessons inward: it does not virtualise Heads and it does not define
a full L2 ledger, but it does require compact factory rights to remain
enforceable, ordered, and payable.

\section{Layering and Positioning}
\label{sec:layering}

Lerna stays inside the Cardano/Hydra settlement vocabulary. Cardano L1 supplies
eUTxO accounting, script-governed outputs, native assets, validity intervals,
and min-ADA rules~\cite{cardano-eutxo}. Hydra supplies the
outer Head lifecycle: open, snapshot, close, contest, and fanout. Lerna supplies
the inner factory discipline used when many compact virtual-channel rights must
be materialised into ordinary ledger outputs. Figure~\ref{fig:stack} summarises
this placement.

\subsection{Lerna below Interhead: from virtual Heads to virtual rights}
\label{sec:below-interhead}

Interhead virtualises Hydra Heads across Heads: it studies how two Heads and
their intermediaries can move a virtual Head state between Head contexts while
preserving adjudication conditions~\cite{interhead}. Lerna operates at the next
smaller granularity. It does not create a virtual Head and does not specify
cross-Head transport. Instead, it virtualises rights and channels inside one
physical Head, or inside a virtual Head supplied by an Interhead construction.

The distinction is important. A virtual Head still eventually exposes a Head
state to Hydra-like closing and settlement. Lerna asks what happens inside that
state when a factory has compressed many bilateral or application-specific
rights into a small number of Head UTxOs. The answer is factory-local: the
latest Lerna state must bind the Head id, factory id, network id, settlement
epoch, unresolved value, owners, receipts, and materialisation budget. That
state may live as compact Head-local accounting, or it may be represented by a
claim UTxO when explicit ledger settlement is required. In both cases, Lerna is
below Interhead: Interhead says where the virtual Head may be; Lerna says how
rights inside that Head are released.

\subsection{Inner rollout before outer rollout: Lerna and Hydrozoa}
\label{sec:inner-rollout}

Hydrozoa separates execution from custody and gives deterministic rollout or
fallback procedures for turning L2 ledger effects into L1 transactions when
large withdrawal sets must be paid out~\cite{hydrozoa-spec}. Lerna borrows the
design ethic, not the ledger architecture. It treats compact factory rights as
an inner state that must be deterministically rolled out before the outer
system is asked to carry custody.

This creates a simple ordering rule. First, materialise the compact Lerna
factory state into ordinary Head UTxOs or into a bounded continuation claim.
Only then rely on Hydra fanout, Interhead movement, or Hydrozoa-style rollout
for the outer layer. Without this inner rollout, the outer layer sees a small
UTxO whose datum names a compressed factory, not the individual rights that
users expect to receive. Lerna's claim-materialisation profile instantiates
this rule: it turns a compact claim into selected settlement outputs, release
receipts, and, if necessary, one continuation claim whose
remaining value and frontier are explicit.

\subsection{Why compact factory state matters for Hydra fanout}
\label{sec:compact-fanout}

Hydra fanout is the final exposure point of a Head state. The Hydra
documentation records practical constraints around Head finalisation: large or
complex UTxO sets can exceed fanout capacity, and tokens minted but not burned
inside an open Head can prevent finalisation~\cite{hydra-known}. These are not
abstract inconveniences for factories. A naive virtual-channel factory that
keeps every right as a separate Head UTxO can create fanout pressure exactly
when the system is least able to negotiate new structure. A factory that mints
control tokens internally and leaves them unresolved can create a finalisation
hazard.

Lerna's compactness is a direct response to that pressure. During ordinary
operation, the Head carries a small factory object rather than a fanout-sized
collection of user outputs. Compactness, however, is debt. Before the Head is
closed, contested, fanned out, or rolled into another custody context, Lerna
must repay the debt by deterministic materialisation: selected rights become
ordinary outputs, release receipts advance, remaining rights are represented by
one bounded continuation, and the value equation is checked exactly over
lovelace and native assets. Compact state is therefore acceptable only because
the protocol specifies how it becomes non-compact again.

The token rule is equally strict. If Lerna uses factory, receipt, or control
tokens inside a Head, those tokens must either be burned before finalisation or
be represented as ordinary conserved assets in the materialised state. Compact
factory accounting is not allowed to hide a minted-token finalisation hazard.

\subsection[Compatibility across host ledgers]{Compatibility with physical Heads, Interhead virtual Heads,\\and Hydrozoa-style ledgers}
\label{sec:compatibility}

Lerna is compatible with three host settings.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=3.8cm,style=nextline]
\item[Physical Hydra Head.]
The Head-local profile stores compact factory state directly in the Head
ledger. The claim-materialisation profile introduces a claim UTxO when the
factory needs script-controlled release, reduced-signature settlement, or
multi-batch continuation before outer fanout.

\item[Interhead virtual Head.]
Interhead may supply the virtual Head context. Lerna does not alter the
Interhead conversion or punish phases. It binds its own rights state to the
virtual Head's identity and requires inner materialisation before that state is
treated as settled across Head boundaries.

\item[Hydrozoa-style ledger.]
A Hydrozoa-style system may provide deterministic outer rollout and
rule-based fallback. Lerna can be used as an inner rights factory whose compact
state is expanded into ledger outputs before the Hydrozoa rollout algorithm is
asked to distribute custody.
\end{description}

The compatibility claim is deliberately modest. Lerna assumes the host system
has a notion of ledger outputs, value conservation, domain separation,
deadlines, and a way to submit deterministic materialisation transactions. It
does not require the host system to adopt one particular claim script, and it
does not require Lerna to adopt the host system's full dispute game.

\subsection{What Lerna deliberately does not take from each prior work}
\label{sec:prior-work-boundaries}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=3.8cm,style=nextline]
\item[From Hydra Head.]
Lerna takes the Head lifecycle as substrate. It does not replace open, close,
contest, or fanout, and it cannot rescue a stale Head close that no authorised
actor contests in time.

\item[From Interhead.]
Lerna takes the idea that Head contexts can themselves be virtualised. It does
not take over Interhead's cross-Head conversion protocol or intermediary
aggregation. Its unit is a right inside a Head, not a Head across Heads.

\item[From Hydrozoa.]
Lerna takes the discipline of deterministic rollout when compact L2 state must
be exposed to custody. It does not adopt Hydrozoa's execution/custody split as
its own ledger model and is not a competing state-channel architecture.

\item[From eltoo, factories, and Perun.]
Lerna takes the lesson that latest-state replacement and factory adjudication
should avoid punitive games when possible~\cite{eltoo,factories,perun}. It
does not claim to be an eltoo construction for Cardano, and it does not import
Bitcoin-style update transactions into the eUTxO setting.
\end{description}

\section{System and Threat Model}
\label{sec:system}

\subsection{Host Interface}
\label{sec:host-interface}

Lerna is intentionally specified below the host lifecycle. A host Head,
virtual Head, or compatible ledger environment must provide the following
abstract interface:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=3.2cm,style=nextline]
\item[Scoped identity.]
A stable domain, network, Head, factory, and epoch namespace for every compact
state and materialisation witness.

\item[Authenticated state evidence.]
A way to recognise the latest same-scope state, either as signed Head evidence
or as a ledger-visible claim datum and transition witness.

\item[Ledger accounting.]
Explicit outputs, values, native assets, validity intervals, and transaction
budgets sufficient to check exact conservation and admission bounds.

\item[Submission path.]
At least one authorised path by which admissible materialisation, continuation,
or final settlement can reach the host ledger within the relevant deadlines.
\end{description}

The host interface is a contract between the thesis and its environment. If an
environment cannot provide one of these capabilities, Lerna may still be a useful
local accounting convention, but the settlement safety claim in this paper does
not apply.

\subsection{Actors}
\label{sec:actors}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.8cm,style=nextline]
\item[Head participant.]
A Hydra party able to run a Hydra node, sign snapshots, close, and contest.

\item[Virtual participant.]
An application endpoint whose right is represented in Lerna factory state. A
virtual participant may or may not be a Head participant.

\item[Operator.]
An application actor that can coordinate batching, submit transactions, or fund
ordinary fees. The operator does not gain authority to redirect factory value.

\item[Watcher.]
An actor that observes Head lifecycle events and Lerna evidence, and can cause
fresh evidence or settlement transactions to be submitted through an authorised
path.
\end{description}

\subsection{Adversary}
\label{sec:adversary}

The adversary may corrupt participants, operators, watchers, or Head
participants; withhold signatures; provide stale latest-state evidence; propose
same-number conflicting states; withhold proof bodies; try replay across
domains, networks, Heads, factories, or epochs; create outputs below min-ADA;
over-release lovelace or native assets; reorder multi-batch settlement; mutate
continuation datums; leave minted control tokens unresolved; force large
fanout sets; or tamper with evidence used for materialisation acceptance.

\subsection{Assumptions}
\label{sec:assumptions}

\begin{assumption}[Hydra contestability]
\label{asm:hydra-contestability}
If the Head is closed with stale state, at least one honest actor can observe
the close and contest with newer valid Head evidence before the contestation
deadline.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Cardano ledger validation]
\label{asm:ledger-validation}
Submitted materialisation, claim-creation, claim-settlement, and continuation
transactions are validated by the Cardano ledger according to the applicable
ledger rules.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Head-local checkpoint or materialisation]
\label{asm:head-local-checkpoint}
When the Head-local factory profile is used, safety requires at least one
authorised Head participant, operator, or watcher to ensure that the latest
Lerna state is represented in a signed Hydra snapshot before the Head is
closed, or to contest a stale close with an already-signed newer snapshot before
the relevant contestation period expires. Lerna does not assume that fresh Head
transactions can be inserted after close. If this liveness and evidence
availability assumption is unacceptable for the application or deployment
environment, the design must use the claim-materialisation profile rather than
relying on Head-local accounting alone.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Proof availability]
\label{asm:proof-availability}
Proof bodies committed by the proof witness root are available to the verifier
before materialisation is accepted.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Watcher and operator liveness]
\label{asm:liveness}
At least one actor with sufficient evidence and authority can submit accepted
materialisation plans within the relevant Head and ledger deadlines. This is a
liveness assumption, not a claim that Lerna can force offline parties to act.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Bounded materialisation]
\label{asm:bounded-materialisation}
The number of outputs, proof bytes, execution units, and continuation value fit
within the budget committed in the factory state or claim datum.
\end{assumption}

\section{Factory and Claim State}
\label{sec:state}

\begin{definition}[Settlement scope]
\label{def:scope}
A Lerna settlement scope is the tuple
\[
  \Sigma=(\mathsf{domain},\mathsf{networkId},\mathsf{hydraHeadId},
  \mathsf{factoryId},\mathsf{claimEpoch}).
\]
Every latest-state header, transition, receipt, proof-availability commitment,
and non-interference proof is interpreted only inside this scope.
\end{definition}

datum and checked at the ledger boundary.

\begin{definition}[Factory state]
\label{def:factory-state}
A Lerna factory state \(F\) is a compact Head-local object:
\[
\begin{aligned}
F = (&\Sigma,\mathsf{latestStateNumber},\mathsf{rightsCommitment},
\mathsf{unresolvedValue},\\
&\mathsf{signerPolicy},\mathsf{releaseReceiptRoot},
\mathsf{settledRightFrontier},\mathsf{materialisationBudget}).
\end{aligned}
\]
The rights commitment is a commitment to the ordered rights frame: each right's
owner, value, application channel id, and release condition. The unresolved
value is exact over lovelace and native assets. The frontier is the greatest
canonical right prefix already materialised or released.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Head snapshot embedding]
\label{def:head-snapshot-embedding}
A Head-local factory state is embedded when a Hydra snapshot contains one or
more Head UTxOs whose datum and value commit to \(F\). A newer embedded factory
state supersedes an older one only if it has the same settlement scope and a
strictly greater latest-state number. A materialised embedding replaces the
compact factory UTxO set by ordinary Head UTxOs for selected rights plus, if
needed, one compact continuation whose frontier and unresolved value are
advanced deterministically.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Head-local release safety]
\label{lem:head-local-safety}
Assume Hydra contestability, signed-snapshot availability, and bounded
materialisation. If the closed or successfully contested Head snapshot embeds
the greatest signed Lerna factory state \(F\), then deterministic
materialisation of \(F\) cannot release more value than \(F.\mathsf{unresolvedValue}\),
cannot release a right at or below \(F.\mathsf{settledRightFrontier}\), and cannot
change rights outside the authorised non-interference frame.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The signed Hydra snapshot selects the enforceable Head UTxO set. Scope binding
prevents replay of a factory state across domains, networks, Heads, factories,
or settlement epochs. Strict latest-state supersession selects one factory
state among same-scope candidates. Canonical frontier advancement excludes
already-released rights. Exact value conservation requires selected outputs
plus any compact continuation to equal \(F.\mathsf{unresolvedValue}\). The
non-interference frame restricts reduced-signature materialisation to signed
owners and preserves the untouched rights commitment. The lemma is conditional:
if the greatest signed factory state is not available for close or contest, the
Head-local profile does not provide this safety envelope.
\end{proof}

The claim-materialisation profile uses the following domain separators:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
\textbf{Domain} & \textbf{Purpose}\\
\midrule
\texttt{Lerna:ClaimUTxO:LatestStateHeader} & latest-state and transition binding\\
\texttt{Lerna:ClaimUTxO:ReleaseReceipt} & release receipt nullifiers\\
\texttt{Lerna:ClaimUTxO:ProofAvailability} & proof witness package binding\\
\texttt{Lerna:ClaimUTxO:NonInterferenceProof} & reduced-signature frame proof\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{definition}[Claim datum]
\label{def:claim-datum}
A claim datum \(C\) is the ledger representation of compact factory rights in
the claim-materialisation profile. It contains:
\[
\begin{aligned}
C = (&\mathsf{factoryId},\mathsf{hydraHeadId},\mathsf{claimEpoch},
\mathsf{networkId},\mathsf{domain},\mathsf{latestStateNumber},\\
&\mathsf{stateCommitment},\mathsf{releaseReceiptRoot},
\mathsf{nonInterferenceRoot},\mathsf{authorisationMode},\\
&\mathsf{unresolvedLovelace},\mathsf{unresolvedAssets},
\mathsf{minAdaFloor},\mathsf{requiredSigners},\\
&\mathsf{settledRightCount},\mathsf{lastSettledRightId},
\mathsf{maxOutputCount},\mathsf{maxProofSizeBytes},\\
&\mathsf{maxExecutionMemory},\mathsf{maxExecutionSteps},
\mathsf{proofAvailabilityMode},\mathsf{proofWitnessRoot}).
\end{aligned}
\]
The state commitment is recomputed from length-prefixed canonical fields, not
accepted as an independent assertion.
\end{definition}

The claim value is:
\[
  \Value(C)=
  \mathsf{lovelace}(C.\mathsf{unresolvedLovelace})
  + C.\mathsf{unresolvedAssets}.
\]

The Head-local profile uses the same conceptual fields, but their enforcement
comes from the Head snapshot discipline and the authorised
checkpoint/materialisation assumption rather than from spending a claim UTxO on
L1. This is the main reason the paper treats the two settlement profiles as
safety envelopes rather than as deployment stages.

\label{sec:state-machine}

Lerna is easiest to audit as a small state machine around compactness debt. The
state machine is not a new host-ledger lifecycle. It is the inner discipline
that decides whether a compact factory state is still safely compact, has been
checkpointed inside a Head, or must be materialised through a claim object.

\section{Materialisation Transition}
\label{sec:transition}

A materialisation transition selects a canonical batch of rights
\(R=(r_1,\ldots,r_k)\) with matching settlement outputs
\(O=(o_1,\ldots,o_k)\). In the claim-materialisation profile, the transition
spends the current claim UTxO, provides a redeemer \(D\), an authorised signer
set \(S\), and produces either a final zero-claim result or a continuation
claim datum \(C'\). In the Head-local profile, the same transition discipline
must be reflected in the Head snapshot before the outer Hydra contestation
window can be relied upon. Figure~\ref{fig:claim-transition} gives the
claim-materialisation view of the same transition.

\subsection{Admission}
\label{sec:admission}

The transition is admitted only if:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.2cm]
\item \(D.\mathsf{nextStateNumber} > C.\mathsf{latestStateNumber}\).
\item The domain, network id, Head id, factory id, and claim epoch in the
redeemer match the datum.
\item \(k > 0\) and \(k \le C.\mathsf{maxOutputCount}\).
\item Every selected output is at or above \(C.\mathsf{minAdaFloor}\).
\item Proof size and execution-cost evidence are within the committed bounds.
\item The batch starts strictly after \(C.\mathsf{lastSettledRightId}\) in the
canonical settled-right order.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Authorisation}
\label{sec:authorisation}

Lerna specifies two authorisation modes. In full authorisation, the settlement
evidence must contain exactly the datum-bound signer set. In reduced-signature
mode, the authorised signers must be an exact proper subset and the
non-interference witness must show that all materialised outputs belong to
signed owners and that the untouched frame is preserved. This matters because
reduced-signature settlement is a common source of accidental over-claiming:
without a frame proof, an output for an unsigned owner could be smuggled into a
partial settlement.

\subsection{Conservation}
\label{sec:conservation}

Let \(M\) be the value materialised by the selected settlement outputs and
\(Remainder\) be the value recorded in the continuation claim, or zero for a
final settlement. The transition relation requires:
\[
  \Value(C) = M + Remainder.
\]
This equality is exact over lovelace and native assets. Native-asset treatment
is therefore part of the protocol invariant, not an optional textual extension.

\subsection{Release Receipts}
\label{sec:release-receipts}

For each materialised right \(r_i\) and selected output \(o_i\), Lerna emits a
deterministic receipt:
\[
\Receipt_i=\hash(\texttt{Lerna:ClaimUTxO:ReleaseReceipt},
  \Sigma, r_i.\mathsf{rightId}, \mathsf{desc}(o_i), i).
\]
The transition advances the receipt root from the previous root to a new root
covering the settled batch. A later settlement cannot materialise the same
right prefix again because the canonical frontier and receipt root must both
advance.

\subsection{Continuation}
\label{sec:continuation}

If unresolved value remains, exactly one continuing output must return to the
claim address with continuing state \(C'\). That state must preserve the
scope, signer policy, non-interference root, materialisation budget, proof
availability mode, and proof witness root, while advancing the latest-state
number, receipt root, settled-right count, last settled right id, unresolved
value, and state commitment. If no unresolved value remains, there must be no
continuing claim output.

\section{Ledger Enforcement}
\label{sec:enforcement}

The claim-materialisation profile is intended to be enforced at the ledger
boundary by a claim script or equivalent admissibility rule. Its key checks are:

\begin{longtable}{L{0.33\linewidth}L{0.57\linewidth}}
\caption{Ledger checks required by the claim-materialisation profile.}
\\
\toprule
\textbf{Check family} & \textbf{Ledger responsibility}\\
\midrule
\endhead
Input and continuation & Resolve the own input, compare its value to claim
value, require either no continuation for final settlement or exactly one
inline-datum continuation.\\
Identity and domain & Bind datum and redeemer to the same factory, Head,
network, epoch, and latest-state domain.\\
Latest state & Require the old commitment to match the datum and the next state
number to be strictly greater.\\
State commitment & Recompute the current and next state commitments from
canonical fields.\\
Receipt progress & Recompute the expected release-receipt root and require it
to advance.\\
Authorisation & Check exact full signers or exact reduced-signature
non-interference signers.\\
Conservation & Require input claim value to equal selected outputs plus
continuation value across lovelace and native assets.\\
Output descriptors & Bind selected output indices, payment key hashes,
lovelace, and asset bundles to the redeemer descriptors.\\
Admission bounds & Enforce positive numeric bounds, output-count bounds,
min-ADA policy, and continuation-value safety.\\
\bottomrule
\end{longtable}

These checks are intentionally redundant with the deterministic transition
model in \Cref{sec:transition}. The model explains the expected transition;
ledger enforcement decides whether a claim spend is admissible; external
evidence, where used, should bind the accepted transition to the same scope,
value equation, and output descriptors.

\section{Acceptance Argument for the Claim-Materialisation Profile}
\label{sec:acceptance}

\begin{lemma}[Scope and supersession]
\label{lem:scope-supersession}
For the claim-materialisation profile, a settlement transaction cannot validly
mix evidence across domains, networks, Heads, factories, or settlement epochs,
and cannot settle a state number less than or equal to the datum-bound latest
state number.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The claim state and transition witness carry the same settlement scope, and
ledger enforcement checks equality of the scope fields. The next state number
is required to be strictly greater than the datum's latest-state number, while
the current and next commitments are recomputed from canonical fields.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Conservation and bounded continuation]
\label{lem:conservation-continuation}
Every accepted claim-materialisation transition conserves the claim input value
exactly across selected outputs and, if present, the unique continuation claim.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Ledger enforcement compares the input claim value with the sum of selected
settlement outputs plus continuation value. The equality is over lovelace and
native assets. Admission checks bound the number of selected outputs, proof
size, execution units, and min-ADA floor.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Receipt frontier]
\label{lem:receipt-frontier}
An accepted transition cannot materialise the same canonical right prefix
twice.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The transition must start strictly after the previous
\(\mathsf{lastSettledRightId}\), recompute the release-receipt root for the
selected batch, and advance both the receipt root and the settled-right
frontier. A replayed or reordered prefix fails the frontier or receipt-root
check.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Reduced-signature non-interference]
\label{lem:noninterference}
In reduced-signature mode, an accepted transition can affect only rights whose
owners are represented by the authorised signer subset.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The non-interference witness binds the authorised signer subset, touched
participants, selected right ids, settlement output descriptors, and the
untouched frame. If an unsigned owner's output is inserted or an untouched
right is changed, the witness no longer matches the datum-bound
non-interference root.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[No-stranding]
\label{lem:no-stranding}
For every accepted claim-materialisation transition, unresolved value is either
paid to selected settlement outputs, preserved in exactly one same-scope
continuation claim, or absent because final settlement has reached zero
unresolved value.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\Cref{lem:conservation-continuation} gives exact value conservation and requires
the continuing value to equal the input claim value minus selected outputs.
\Cref{sec:continuation} requires exactly one continuation when unresolved value
remains and no continuation when it does not. Scope preservation and monotone
frontier advancement prevent the remaining value from being represented under a
different claim identity or a regressed batch boundary.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Claim-materialisation safety theorem]
\label{thm:claim-materialisation-safety}
For any accepted claim-materialisation settlement, if the ledger validates the
claim spend and the supplied evidence satisfies the transition obligations
below, then the settlement:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.2cm]
\item spends the script-controlled claim UTxO under the claim admissibility
rule;
\item binds latest-state evidence to one domain, network id, Head id, factory
id, and claim epoch;
\item strictly supersedes the previous latest-state number;
\item preserves recomputable datum, transition, and continuation commitments;
\item uses exact full authorisation or reduced-signature non-interference;
\item conserves lovelace and native assets across selected outputs and
continuation state;
\item emits deterministic release receipts and advances the settled-right
frontier;
\item leaves no unresolved value stranded outside selected outputs or a
same-scope continuation;
\item respects committed output-count, proof-size, execution-unit, and min-ADA
bounds;
\item binds proof availability before materialisation;
\item accepts only evidence that is bound to the same scope, value equation,
transition, and selected outputs; and
\item reaches profile acceptance only when no unresolved claim value remains.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

The theorem should be read as a conditional acceptance theorem for the
claim-materialisation profile, not as a free-standing proof of the whole Lerna
design. It is intentionally narrow: the claim holds only where the host ledger
validates the spend, \Cref{asm:proof-availability,asm:liveness} hold, and the
materialised batch remains within the committed budget of
\Cref{asm:bounded-materialisation}.

\begin{longtable}{L{0.38\linewidth}L{0.52\linewidth}}
\caption{Acceptance obligations for \Cref{thm:claim-materialisation-safety}.}
\\
\toprule
\textbf{Obligation} & \textbf{Meaning}\\
\midrule
\endhead
Ledger enforcement & Claim spend follows the admissibility rule for the active claim state\\
Latest-state supersession & Same-scope settlement strictly advances state number\\
State-commitment integrity & Commitments are recomputable from canonical fields\\
Exact authorisation & Full or reduced signer evidence matches the datum policy\\
Non-interference frame & Reduced settlement affects only signed owners\\
Asset conservation & Lovelace and native assets are exactly conserved\\
Receipt nullification & Materialised rights cannot be claimed twice\\
Multi-batch progress & Partial batches advance a canonical frontier\\
No-stranding & Remaining value is either paid, preserved in one continuation, or absent\\
Admission bounds & Output count, proof size, costs, and min-ADA are bounded\\
Proof availability & Witness availability is bound before materialisation\\
Evidence binding & Ledger observations and transition witnesses bind to the same scope\\
Final acceptance & Final settlement leaves no unresolved claim value\\
\bottomrule
\end{longtable}

general state-channel framework. Its contribution is the rule that compact
factory rights must be repayable by deterministic materialisation before outer
settlement machinery is asked to preserve custody.

\section{Limitations and Open Questions}
\label{sec:limitations}

The unified profile framing removes status language; it does not
remove the safety boundary. Lerna remains bounded in the following ways.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.2cm]
\item Instantiation evidence is corroborative; it is not a deployment claim.
\item The theorem assumes Cardano ledger validation and Hydra lifecycle
observations behave according to their stated rules.
\item The Head-local profile is safe only under the authorised
checkpoint/materialisation assumption stated above. If that assumption is too
strong, the application must use claim-materialisation rather than compact
Head-local accounting alone.
\item Proof bodies committed by the proof witness root must be available to the
verifier.
\item Watcher/operator liveness remains a real deployment assumption.
\item Materialisation is safe only within the committed output-count, proof,
execution-unit, and min-ADA budget.
\item Dynamic membership, arbitrary application state, policy-level mint/burn
logic, and cross-Head settlement composition are outside the accepted
claim-materialisation profile.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Open Questions}
\label{sec:open-questions}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=3.4cm,style=nextline]
\item[Head-local formalisation.]
Can the Head-local profile be given a transition relation and liveness theorem
with the same precision as the claim-materialisation profile?

\item[Proof availability.]
Which storage and retrieval mechanism should make proof bodies available before
materialisation deadlines, and how should non-availability be surfaced to
watchers?

\item[Capacity envelopes.]
What are the practical output-count, proof-size, execution-cost, and min-ADA
envelopes for representative factory sizes on the intended host ledger?

\item[Application semantics.]
Which application-level rights can safely be represented by the factory-local
non-interference frame without adding an application-specific proof system?

\item[Cross-Head composition.]
When an Interhead or Hydrozoa-style host changes the outer settlement context,
what extra binding is needed so Lerna's inner receipts and frontiers remain
unambiguous?
\end{description}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Lerna's position is precise when it is described through the safety envelopes in
\Cref{sec:profile-boundary}. Interhead virtualises Heads across Heads; Lerna
virtualises rights and channels inside a Head or virtual Head. Hydrozoa shows
why execution and custody should be separated and why rollout must be
deterministic; Lerna applies that ethic to the inner materialisation of compact
factory rights. Hydra Head supplies the close/contest/fanout substrate and the
practical constraints that make compactness valuable; Lerna repays compactness
through deterministic materialisation, release receipts, non-interference
checks, and exact value conservation.

Claim-materialisation is therefore one strong settlement profile, not the whole
identity of the protocol. The Head-local factory profile remains useful when its
liveness assumption is acceptable. The claim-materialisation profile is
appropriate when that assumption is too strong and explicit ledger settlement is
required. In both profiles, the academic claim is the same: compactness is safe
only when enforceable state, deterministic payout order, balance preservation,
and no-stranding are made explicit. The remaining work follows directly from
\Cref{sec:limitations,sec:open-questions}: formalise the Head-local transition
relation, make proof availability operational rather than merely committed,
measure real ledger limits, and state every deployment assumption at the same
precision as the protocol checks it.
